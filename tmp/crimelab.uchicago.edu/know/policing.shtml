Policing

Whether putting more police on the street helps reduce crime has also been the subject of considerable debate. One of the best studies of this question comes from William Evans of Notre Dame and Emily Owens of Cornell, who use the launch of the Community Policing Services (COPS) program in the 1990s as a "natural experiment" to show that increased funding to hire additional police officers lead to reductions in crime. Yale Law professor John Donohue and Crime Lab director Jens Ludwig argue that each extra dollar spent on the COPS program might generate as much as $4 to $8.50 in benefits to society, making increased spending on police one of the most cost-effective uses of additional government spending. What is less clear is the degree to which the COPS program works by simply putting more officers on the street, versus potentially also changing the amount of "community policing," which involves efforts to improve police-community cooperation. (For those who are interested in more details about this policing strategy, Wesley Skogan of Northwestern University has written an excellent review of community policing strategies as they have been carried out in Chicago).

Changing the way we deploy police resources might further increase the efficiency of police spending. Particularly promising seem to be those strategies that try to focus law enforcement personnel on the highest-risk people, places and activities. These strategies include problem-oriented policing that concentrate police on high-crime "hot spots", or on high-cost behaviors such as illegal gun carrying , which have been shown to help reduce crime through good randomized or natural experiment studies. As Crime Lab member Dennis Rosenbaum notes, these types of targeted policing strategies have become major elements of policing strategies in many large cities, including Chicago.
