Gangs

In many big cities a large share of drug distribution - and violence - seems to be accounted for by street gangs. While the appropriate definition of what should count as a street gang remains controversial, there is a large descriptive literature documenting the reasons why youth join street gangs, whether or how gangs differ across race and ethnic groups, the evolution of street gangs over time across cities, and the nature of day to day life for gang members. Good examples of this descriptive work includes Malcolm Klein's  The American Street Gang, Irving Spergel's The Youth Gang Problem, and the studies that sociologist Sudhir Venkatesh has done with Crime Lab member Steve Levitt on gangs [1, 2].

What policymakers might do to reduce gang activity, or at least the social harms associated with such activity, remains an open question. A useful catalog of different approaches that have been tried was compiled by our University of Chicago colleague Irving Spergel. Two gang interventions that have received a great deal of public attention, the Boston Gun Project and Chicago's Ceasefire program, place special emphasis on reducing the retaliatory killings by gangs. The Boston Gun Project combined efforts to target gun trafficking and improve access to social services with a group-oriented deterrence approach that holds gangs as a whole responsible when individual members are involved in violent behavior. This last group-oriented deterrence component seems to be applied informally in some police departments already, and ethnographic interviews provide at least suggestive evidence that gang leaders might be responsive to such pressures. Ceasefire in Chicago uses outreach staff to dissuade people from sustaining the cycle of violence, and various public education efforts to try to change social norms around violence. A non-experimental study of Ceasefire finds some encouraging results, although absent a rigorous experimental design there necessarily remains some uncertainty about the degree to which the program itself contributes to any observed changes in crime patterns in targeted neighborhoods.

A number of cities have also experimented with civil gang injunctions, which arise from lawsuits that city or district attorneys file in civil court seeking relief from the public nuisance caused by gang members.  The injunctions prohibit specific persons from engaging in particular activities within clearly defined "target areas".  Crime Lab member Jeffrey Grogger of the University of Chicago evaluated the effects of gang injunctions in Los Angeles County, one of the most gang-plagued regions of the country.  He found that gang injunctions reduced violent crime rates by 5 to 10 percent during the year they were imposed.  He also found that they were relatively inexpensive, implying that their benefit-to-cost ratio is fairly high.
