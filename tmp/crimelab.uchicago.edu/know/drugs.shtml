Drugs

Substance abuse and dependence-and the street market for illicit drugs-are responsible for much property crime and violence perpetrated by drug users, drug sellers, and by others such as robbers who prey upon those involved in illicit drug distribution. A large descriptive literature documents the extent and the impact of violence arising from the drug market. Ethnographic accounts of the drug market, including sociologist Sudhir Venkatesh's Gang Leader for a Day, and Philippe Bourgeois's In Search of Respect, highlight the widespread violence that accompanies the drug trade. These accounts also underscore the often-sophisticated economic structure of the illicit drug market.

Law enforcement seeks to deter or disrupt illicit drug markets in many ways, ranging from the seizure or destruction of Afghan poppy crops, to border controls, to the arrest and incarceration of drug buyers and street-level drug sellers by local law enforcement authorities. The available data suggest the inflation-adjusted price of cocaine and heroin has dropped over the past two decades. Street drugs in Chicago and most other American cities are far cheaper and purer than they were in 1980. These price trends are difficult to interpret, since any impacts of enforcement against the "supply side" of the drug market could be masked by changes in the technology of drug production or distribution. At the very least, it appears that interdiction might have some effect on the cost of drug distribution and sales: Peter Reuter and Crime Lab co-director Harold Pollack estimate that it costs roughly $15,000 to move 1kg of cocaine from Bogota to Miami, while Federal Express would charge less than $100 to move an equivalent legal package. But overall the National Academy of Sciences has argued in a 2001 report that relatively little is currently known about the effectiveness of drug enforcement measures - in part, as Reuter and Pollack note, because policymakers don't require the same type of evidence about enforcement interventions as they do for substance abuse treatment.

Clinical trials suggest that substance abuse treatment has a complicated track record, with some of the best trials done in the area of methadone treatment for opiate users. Most people who go through treatment seem to eventually use again. But treatment is nevertheless quite cost-effective, in the sense that the benefits from treatment (particularly from reductions in criminal behavior by those treated) are large enough to outweigh treatment costs, even though most people go on to use again.
